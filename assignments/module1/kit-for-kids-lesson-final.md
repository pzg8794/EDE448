# Teach a Kit for Kids Lesson: Simulation Draft

## Lesson Context and Requirement Connection

The assignment asks us to use the Organization for Autism Research's Kit for Kids educator resources to teach a 10-minute lesson that helps students support and understand autistic classmates. OAR describes Kit for Kids as a peer-education program for elementary and middle school students. The core story, *What's Up with Nick?*, introduces an autistic student through the perspective of a peer and emphasizes that autistic students may think differently or need accommodations while still being of equal worth. OAR also provides grade-banded friendship tip sheets that focus on being understanding and supportive friends, including communication barriers, sensory differences, and concrete peer actions.

I taught the 10-minute mini-lesson to Grade 2 students in an elementary technology setting connected to coding and robotics routines. I selected this context because my February 26, 2026 TeachingPlacement transcript includes exactly the kind of peer-support situation that OAR's Kit for Kids materials are designed to strengthen: students helping classmates access an activity, students navigating different levels of independence, and a moment where a student questioned whether a visitor counted as a classmate. That real classroom context gave the autism acceptance lesson a concrete problem to practice rather than leaving inclusion as an abstract value.

The lesson connected OAR's grades 2-3 friendship focus to the social routines students already need during technology work. Instead of asking students to identify or discuss anyone's diagnosis, I framed the lesson around what good classmates do when someone communicates, learns, moves, or experiences the room differently. The goal was to help students understand that autistic classmates may need different forms of access, time, space, communication, or support, and that peers can make the classroom more welcoming through specific actions.

## Summary of the 10-Minute Lesson

The lesson was titled *Many Ways to Be a Good Classmate*. I taught it immediately before a partner-based coding and robotics routine. The student-friendly goal was: *I can name two ways to include and support a classmate who communicates, learns, moves, or experiences the room differently.*

I began with a visible first/next/last sequence: first, learn one idea; next, practice two classroom situations; last, choose one action. I explained that autism is one way a person's brain can work and that autistic people are not all the same. Some autistic students may communicate with speech, gestures, writing, pictures, AAC, movement, or silence. Some may need quiet, space, clear directions, time to answer, predictable routines, or a break from noise. I did not ask any child to disclose a diagnosis or name an autistic person they know.

Using the OAR friendship tip sheet, I taught four peer actions:

1. **Invite** a classmate without forcing them.
2. **Wait** so the person has time to communicate.
3. **Ask** respectfully instead of guessing what someone needs.
4. **Respect** sensory needs, personal space, communication tools, breaks, and privacy.

Then I used the placement-based scenario because it came from real classroom life:

> During a technology activity, one student already knows how to get into the program, but several classmates are still stuck. Another student is new to the group and is not following everything yet. What could a supportive classmate do?

Students identified that a supportive classmate could help without taking over, use a calm voice, show one step at a time, wait for the person to try, include the new student as part of the class, and avoid making someone feel bad. One student described showing the next click without grabbing the other person's Chromebook. Another student connected support to feelings by saying that a new person should not be made to feel left out. I closed with a short exit response: *One way I can be a supportive classmate is...*

## Reflection on the Lesson

The lesson showed me that peer education cannot stay abstract. Students often understand kindness in general, but they need concrete language for what kindness looks like during noise, frustration, partner work, technology confusion, and uneven pacing. The strongest teaching moments happened when access broke down: students needed help finding the correct program, some learners finished quickly while others were still circling, and one child initially treated a visitor as outside the class community. Those were not side issues. They were the social conditions of learning.

The strongest part of the lesson was that support happened inside the real activity. I did not pause the class to give a long lecture about respect. I used the live context: if you already know how to get in, help your classmates; if someone is new or not following the routine, do not make them feel bad; if someone is tired or not ready, offer choice and wait. That aligns with the OAR emphasis on actionable peer support. Students need to know that acceptance is not only a nice feeling. It is something they do with their voice, body, materials, space, and patience.

The part I would revise is pacing. Ten minutes was enough time to introduce the friendship actions and practice one scenario, but not enough time for every student to process a new idea about autism, rehearse language with a partner, and connect it to their own behavior. Next time, I would use the same 10-minute lesson as the first step and then revisit the OAR materials across several technology routines. I would also add a simple visual reminder at each table: invite, wait, ask, respect, include.

## Future Classroom Incorporation

In a future classroom, I would use the Kit for Kids materials before partner-heavy activities: robotics, coding, group building, recess transitions, digital publishing, and project showcases. These are moments when students are asked to coordinate language, space, noise, turn-taking, timing, technology, and emotion all at once. That is exactly when misunderstandings about autistic classmates can become exclusion, teasing, taking over, or assuming a student is being difficult.

I would not use the OAR materials as a single story about autism. The Puzzle Plan asks me to combine lived experience, education, data, and research so that I do not reduce students to one label or one behavior. EQUITAS, the equity lens inside that framework, asks whose knowledge is included, what context is missing, and where bias may shape decisions. Through that lens, a peer lesson should not teach students to identify who is autistic. It should teach students to build a classroom where multiple forms of communication and participation are normal.

I would pair the OAR booklet and friendship tips with classroom routines that benefit everyone: first/next/last directions, visual choices, quiet options, partner roles, help cards, wait time, permission to pass, and multiple ways to answer. I would also include autistic-authored perspectives when developmentally appropriate so that students do not learn about autistic people only through non-autistic interpretation. The goal is not pity. The goal is access, dignity, and belonging.

## Conclusion

This lesson reminded me that peer support has to be practiced in ordinary classroom moments: when someone is confused, when the room is loud, when a partner communicates differently, when a new student enters the group, or when a child needs more time. OAR's Kit for Kids materials gave me a clear way to turn those moments into a short, developmentally appropriate peer-education lesson.

For EDE448, my main takeaway is that positive behavioral support includes peer culture. Students with autism and other complex needs should not have to earn belonging by appearing typical. The classroom should already know how to invite, wait, ask, respect, and include. Kit for Kids gives me a concrete way to teach that before harm happens.
