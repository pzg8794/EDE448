## Lesson Plan

**Lesson title:** Many Ways to Be a Good Classmate

**Grade band:** Grades 2-3

**Length:** 10 minutes

**Student-friendly goal:** I can name two ways to include and support an autistic classmate.

**Learning objectives:** By the end of the lesson, the learner will:

1. Explain that autism is one way a person's brain can work and that autistic people are not all the same.
2. Identify at least two supportive peer actions.
3. Apply one supportive action to a familiar classroom scenario.

### Materials

- OAR Kit for Kids grades 2-3 Friendship Tip Sheet
- Selected ideas from OAR's *What's Up with Nick?* booklet
- A visible `FIRST -> NEXT -> LAST` sequence
- Two scenario prompts from this packet
- Optional paper exit response: "One way I can be a supportive classmate is..."

### Neurodiversity And Multiple-Identities Safeguards

- Do not ask whether anyone in the room is autistic or knows an autistic student.
- Do not describe one autistic character as representing every autistic person.
- Use the identity-affirming phrase "autistic student" while respecting that individuals may prefer different language.
- Explain that communication can include speech, gestures, writing, pictures, AAC, movement, or silence.
- Do not require eye contact or infer a person's feelings from facial expression alone. Notice communication, allow processing time, and ask respectfully.
- Do not use the workbook's outdated "one in eighty-eight" statistic.
- Do not use gender binaries, race, language, culture, or another identity as a same/different game. Autism intersects with many identities, and no learner should be asked to disclose a private identity for the lesson.
- Keep the focus on access and peer responsibility: invite, wait, listen, ask, respect, and include. The autistic student should not carry all the work of belonging.

## 10-Minute Teaching Script

### 0:00-1:00 | Preview And Opening

Show the visual sequence:

> First, we will learn one idea. Next, we will practice with two classroom situations. Last, you will choose one action you can use.

Ask:

> What can a good classmate do when another person communicates, learns, moves, or experiences noise differently?

Accept speaking, pointing, writing, or drawing as a response.

### 1:00-3:00 | Autism Without A Single Story

Say:

> Autism is one way a person's brain can work. It can affect communication, senses, routines, interests, movement, and learning. Every autistic person is different. Autistic students have interests, strengths, feelings, and identities just like every other student, and they may need different kinds of access or support.

Check understanding:

> Are all autistic students the same?

Expected response: No.

### 3:00-5:30 | Four Friendship Actions

Use the OAR Friendship Tip Sheet and teach four concrete actions:

1. **Invite:** Ask a classmate to join without forcing them.
2. **Wait:** Give the person time to answer or communicate.
3. **Ask:** If you are unsure what helps, ask instead of guessing.
4. **Respect:** Respect sensory needs, personal space, communication tools, breaks, and privacy.

Say:

> Fair does not always mean everyone gets the exact same thing. Fair can mean each person gets the access they need to participate.

### 5:30-8:30 | Practice With Placement-Informed Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Noisy technology activity**

> During a robotics activity, the room gets loud. A classmate covers their ears and steps away. What could a supportive classmate do?

Listen for: lower the volume, give space, offer a quieter option, keep the path clear, do not tease, and ask what would help.

**Scenario 2: Partner communication**

> During partner coding, a classmate does not answer right away or uses pictures, gestures, writing, or a device. What could a supportive partner do?

Listen for: wait, offer choices, pay attention to the person's communication, share turns, and avoid taking over.

Clarify that support should increase participation and choice, not create dependence or make decisions for someone.

### 8:30-10:00 | Exit Response

Ask the learner to complete one prompt by speaking, pointing, writing, or drawing:

> One way I can be a supportive classmate is...

Follow with:

> Why might that action help?

Close:

> Inclusion is not only being in the same room. It means people can communicate, participate, make choices, and belong.
