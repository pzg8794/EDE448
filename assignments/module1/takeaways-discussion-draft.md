## Draft Response

My biggest takeaway from Module 1 is that the way adults understand developmental differences directly affects the supports students receive. Autism and complex support needs cannot be understood only through diagnosis or behavior checklists. Those tools may help identify needs, but they are incomplete unless educators also consider lived experience, sensory access, communication, culture, family knowledge, and the design of the environment.

This module pushed me to think about the difference between a deficit lens and an access lens. A deficit lens asks, "What is wrong with the student?" An access lens asks, "What is the student communicating, and what is the environment requiring from them?" That shift matters because a student may be struggling with sensory overload, unclear directions, inaccessible communication expectations, or social risk. If adults only see the visible behavior, they may respond with correction when the student actually needs support.

The sensory walk assignment made this concrete. Public and classroom spaces are not neutral. Lighting, sound, crowding, signs, seating, transition demands, and quiet-space options all shape whether a child can participate. The Kit for Kids lesson also reminded me that inclusion is not only an adult responsibility. Peers need language and practice for understanding autism respectfully so autistic students are not forced to carry the whole burden of adaptation.

For my future classroom, I want to build routines that presume competence and make support visible: visual schedules, multiple communication options, sensory breaks, peer inclusion norms, and explicit teaching about difference. My goal is not to make every student look the same. My goal is to help students be understood more accurately and supported with dignity.
