# Sensory Walk Experience

**Piter Garcia**  
**Course:** EDE 448 — Communication and Positive Behavioral Supports for Students with Autism and Other Complex Needs  
**Public space examined:** Pine Brook Elementary School IGNITE computer science classroom  
**Observation context:** Spring 2026 student-teaching placement

## Purpose and Method

For this sensory walk, I examined the IGNITE computer science classroom at Pine Brook Elementary School as a public-school learning environment. I used the Guggenheim’s *Creating a Sensory-Friendly Environment: Becoming a Sensory Detective* resource and Michigan’s Developmental Disabilities Institute’s *Sensory-Friendly Environment Checklist*. The Sensory Detective resource recommends following a visitor’s experience from beginning to end and examining how the environment affects participation. The Michigan checklist adds specific questions about noise, lighting, temperature, crowds, alternative routes, sensory maps, and access to a quiet space.

I therefore considered the classroom from the point of view of a child entering the room, locating materials, listening to directions, participating in a hands-on lesson, communicating a need, taking a break, and transitioning out. I paid attention not only to sight, sound, touch, smell, and taste, but also to movement, body position, and internal regulation. My central question was not whether a child could tolerate the classroom. It was whether the environment gave children enough predictability, choice, communication access, and recovery support to participate with dignity.

## Summary of the Experience

The IGNITE classroom serves elementary students through hands-on computer science, digital literacy, robotics, and engineering activities. Students may use Chromebooks, block-coding programs, robots, building materials, craft supplies, and visual lesson resources. These activities are engaging and offer many ways to learn, but they can also create several forms of sensory input at the same time. Screens provide visual stimulation; devices and peers produce sound; students and materials move around the room; and building activities involve varied textures and fine-motor demands.

The classroom already includes several practices that support sensory access. During my placement, I observed picture-based choices, visual representations of expectations, step-by-step directions, clear start-and-stop signals, structured turn-taking, movement opportunities, and flexible ways for students to respond. These supports increase predictability and reduce the amount of spoken information a child must hold in working memory.

One experience especially shaped my interpretation of sensory-friendliness. A student with complex support needs wanted to keep a laptop nearby while participating in a building activity. Instead of treating the laptop as a distraction or requiring the student to participate in only one approved way, I allowed the student to keep it nearby for comfort while building with blocks. The student remained connected to the activity. This showed me that sensory support is not always a separate room or specialized object. Sometimes it is an adult recognizing a child’s regulation strategy and preserving access without lowering the learning expectation.

## Evidence That the Environment Was Sensory-Friendly

### Visual structure and predictability

The classroom offered visual structure through modeled steps, visual expectations, choice supports, and clear signals for beginning and ending activities. This aligns with the checklist’s emphasis on preparing people for what will happen and reducing uncertainty. For a child who processes spoken language slowly, becomes anxious during transitions, or needs repetition, these supports can prevent confusion from being misread as refusal.

### Multiple ways to participate

The learning design allowed more than one way to participate. Students could build, point, speak, use technology, work with a peer, or use another response method. This flexibility is sensory-friendly because it does not require every child to use the same body position, communication mode, pace, or material. The laptop example demonstrated that a regulation support and an academic task can coexist.

### Movement and regulation opportunities

Movement and sensory breaks were already part of the classroom support system. The Sensory Detective resource explains that sensory-friendly design should enable neurodivergent people to participate rather than requiring them to endure an environment until they become overwhelmed. A break is therefore not a reward for compliance or a punishment for behavior. It is an access support that can help a student regulate and return to learning.

## Evidence of Remaining Sensory Barriers

### Cumulative sound, movement, and visual input

The strongest barrier was the cumulative sensory load during active STEM lessons. A single Chromebook, robot, conversation, or container of blocks may not be overwhelming by itself. However, several devices, peer conversations, moving bodies, visual displays, and tactile materials operating at the same time can make it difficult for a child to determine which information is most important.

A child may cover their ears, move away, hold tightly to a familiar object, repeat a question, stop working, or resist a transition. Those actions may communicate overload, uncertainty, or a need for more processing time rather than a lack of interest or ability. A sensory-detective perspective requires the adult to examine what happened in the environment before interpreting the child’s behavior.

### Supports that depend on adult interpretation

A second barrier was that available supports depended partly on an adult recognizing the need in the moment. Flexible adult responses are valuable, but access is more dependable when students can identify and use supports without waiting for an adult to interpret visible distress. The Michigan checklist asks whether a space offers a sensory map, information about noisy or busy conditions, an alternative route, and access to a quiet area. Those supports make the environment more predictable before a child reaches overload.

### Frequent transitions

A third barrier involved transitions. IGNITE lessons often move between whole-group directions, devices, hands-on materials, peer collaboration, cleanup, and departure. Each transition can change the sound level, movement pattern, visual focus, and expectations. Even when each step is manageable by itself, rapid or unclear transitions can accumulate into sensory and executive-function overload.

## Overall Assessment

I would describe the IGNITE classroom as meaningfully supportive and **partially sensory-friendly**, with strong inclusive teaching practices already present. Its visual supports, flexible participation, movement options, and responsive adults create important access. At the same time, sensory-friendliness should not depend only on individual teachers noticing distress and improvising a solution. The environment would become more consistently accessible if sensory information, reset options, and choices were visible to every student from the beginning of the lesson.

## Recommendations

### 1. Create a visual sensory story and classroom map

A short, child-friendly visual guide could show what students may see, hear, touch, and do from entry through dismissal. It could identify the Chromebook area, robotics or building area, supply locations, louder activity zones, seating choices, and the place to take a break. Like the sensory icons in accessible children’s materials, the guide could pair simple images with brief language such as “devices may make sounds,” “materials may feel different,” and “you may ask for headphones or a break.” This would directly apply the Sensory Detective approach and the Michigan checklist’s recommendation for sensory maps and stories.

### 2. Establish a clearly identified low-sensory reset option

The classroom should have a consistent area where a student can reduce sensory input without being excluded from learning. It could include softer lighting when possible, uncluttered seating, noise-reducing headphones, a visual timer, and a small selection of regulation tools. Students should be taught that using the area is a normal self-advocacy choice, not evidence of misbehavior. A portable version could also be used when a permanent area is unavailable.

### 3. Build sensory choices into every lesson before difficulty appears

Lesson previews should identify likely sensory demands and offer choices in advance. Students could choose muted device audio, headphones, a quieter seat, a reduced-material workspace, an alternate texture, additional transition time, or a visual version of verbal directions. Teachers could also stagger material distribution and cleanup to reduce crowding and competing noise. Providing these choices universally would reduce the need for students to display distress before receiving support.

## Connection to the Course Theme

This experience connects to the course theme of communication as access to agency. Children are often understood through the behavior adults can see, while the environmental conditions producing that behavior remain unexamined. If a student leaves a group, keeps a familiar device nearby, avoids a material, or needs repeated directions, the first question should not be, “How do we make the student comply?” A stronger question is, “What is the student communicating, what sensory or access barrier is present, and what support would expand participation?”

This also connects to my Puzzle Plan and EQUITAS framework. Shallow evidence records only the visible behavior and risks turning an access barrier into a judgment about competence. Richer evidence includes sensory load, communication mode, predictability, student preference, cultural and family knowledge, and the design of the environment. A sensory-friendly classroom does not ask children to become less complex in order to belong. It redesigns the conditions so that more children can participate, communicate, and learn with dignity.
