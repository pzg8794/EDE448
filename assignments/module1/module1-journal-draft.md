# Module 1 Journal Draft

## Prompt

How does the world come to understand developmental differences?

## Submission Draft

The world often comes to understand developmental differences through the systems that have the most power to name them: schools, medicine, psychology, media, and family/community experience. One thing I am taking from this module is that those systems can either help people gain access to support or reduce a person to a label. Autism is a useful example because it is often described through diagnostic categories, social communication differences, sensory differences, and support needs, but autistic people also describe autism through identity, culture, relationships, and lived experience.

For me, the most important shift is moving from "What is wrong with this student?" to "What is this student communicating, and what is the environment asking of them?" That shift matters because developmental differences are not only inside the child. They are also shaped by the classroom, the sensory environment, the expectations for communication, the tools available, the language adults use, and whether a student is allowed to show competence in more than one way.

The educator-facing readings help me see why teachers need accurate knowledge about autism and complex support needs. Teachers should understand sensory processing, communication differences, executive functioning, social interpretation, and the need for predictable routines. At the same time, a purely clinical or checklist-based view can miss the student as a whole person. A student may need visual supports, AAC, additional processing time, movement, explicit expectations, or sensory options, but those supports should be connected to dignity and agency rather than compliance alone.

The neurodiversity and lived-experience perspective complicates the idea that autism is only a disorder to be corrected. It asks educators to notice how schools decide which behaviors count as "appropriate," which forms of communication count as intelligent, and which students are treated as capable before they have to prove themselves. This connects directly to my Puzzle Plan work because diagnostic and educational systems both create data about people. If the system only records deficits, it will design deficit-based interventions. If the system records context, barriers, strengths, communication attempts, and access needs, it can design supports that are more just.

In my future classroom, I want to understand developmental differences by combining multiple forms of evidence: student voice, family knowledge, direct observation, sensory/environmental information, communication supports, and respectful collaboration with specialists. I also want to be careful about the language I use. If I describe a student as "noncompliant," I may stop looking for the unmet need or blocked communication. If I describe the situation as "the current routine is not giving the student an accessible way to understand, respond, or regulate," I have a better chance of changing the environment and my teaching.

My answer to the essential question is that the world comes to understand developmental differences through stories, systems, labels, relationships, and design choices. As educators, we cannot control all of those systems, but we can decide whether our classroom makes students easier to misunderstand or easier to support.
