## Ready-To-Submit Response

The world often comes to understand developmental differences through the systems that have the most power to name them: schools, medicine, psychology, media, family experience, advocacy organizations, and autistic people themselves. One thing I am taking from Module 1 is that those systems can either help people gain access to support or reduce a person to a label. Autism is a useful example because it is often described through diagnostic categories, social communication differences, sensory differences, and support needs, but autistic people and families also describe autism through identity, culture, relationships, strengths, barriers, and lived experience.

For me, the most important shift is moving from "What is wrong with this student?" to "What is this student communicating, and what is the environment asking of them?" That shift matters because developmental differences are not only inside the child. They are also shaped by the classroom, sensory environment, expectations for communication, tools available, peer culture, adult language, and whether a student is allowed to show competence in more than one way.

The educator-facing readings help me see why teachers need accurate knowledge about autism and complex support needs. Teachers should understand sensory processing, communication differences, executive functioning, social interpretation, transition needs, and the need for predictable routines. At the same time, a purely clinical or checklist-based view can miss the student as a whole person. A student may need visual supports, AAC, additional processing time, movement, explicit expectations, sensory options, or peer support, but those supports should be connected to dignity and agency rather than compliance alone.

The neurodiversity perspective complicates the idea that autism is only a disorder to be corrected. It asks educators to notice how schools decide which behaviors count as appropriate, which forms of communication count as intelligent, and which students are treated as capable before they have to prove themselves. This connects directly to my Puzzle Plan work because diagnostic and educational systems both create data about people. If the system only records deficits, it will design deficit-based interventions. If the system records context, barriers, strengths, communication attempts, sensory load, and access needs, it can design supports that are more just.

The media source I chose, *Autism: Insight from Inside*, reinforced this tension between labels as access and labels as limitation. One speaker explained that a diagnosis can open access to services and community supports, but it can also carry stigma and make people treat one word as if it explains the whole person. Another part that stood out to me was Temple Grandin's emphasis on building areas of strength instead of treating autistic students as one identical group. That challenged me to think about how easily schools can overgeneralize from a label. Two students may share a diagnosis and still have completely different sensory profiles, communication needs, strengths, interests, and support systems.

My answer to the essential question is that the world comes to understand developmental differences through stories, labels, systems, relationships, and design choices. A label can be useful when it opens support, but it becomes harmful when adults stop looking for the person behind the label. As educators, we cannot control every system that shapes how disability is understood, but we can decide whether our classrooms make students easier to misunderstand or easier to support.

In my future classroom, I want to understand developmental differences by combining multiple forms of evidence: student voice, family knowledge, direct observation, sensory and environmental information, communication supports, peer dynamics, and respectful collaboration with specialists. I also want to be careful about the language I use. If I describe a student as noncompliant, I may stop looking for the unmet need or blocked communication. If I describe the situation as "the current routine is not giving the student an accessible way to understand, respond, or regulate," I have a better chance of changing the environment and my teaching.

EDE448 is helping me frame developmental differences as part of a larger access question: how do we build classrooms where students with autism and complex support needs can communicate, participate, belong, and be believed?
