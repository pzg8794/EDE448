### Who Gets To Name, Interpret, And Design?

The world comes to understand developmental differences through systems of interpretation. Medicine and psychology create diagnostic categories. Schools translate those categories into eligibility, instruction, discipline, and support. Families contribute histories and knowledge that may not be visible during an assessment. Media creates public stories about who autistic people are. Advocacy communities and autistic people themselves challenge, revise, or reclaim those stories. None of these systems is neutral. Each makes some parts of a person visible while risking that other parts will disappear. My central learning from Module 1 is therefore that developmental difference is simultaneously neurological, lived, relational, cultural, and socially interpreted.

Cole and Telesford (2023) provide the clinical and educational language commonly used to describe autism, including differences in social communication, interaction, sensory processing, routines, and focused interests. That language can be useful because it gives families and professionals a shared vocabulary and can open access to services. However, the chapter also makes clear that autism is heterogeneous and that identification relies on caregiver reports and professional observation rather than one biological test. A diagnostic label can organize support, but it cannot tell a teacher what a particular learner understands, prefers, fears, enjoys, or needs in a specific moment. The level of support a person needs can also change across activities and environments. Diagnosis is therefore one source of evidence, not a complete explanation of a person.

Ortega's (2013) discussion of the neurodiversity movement helped me understand why labels have both institutional and identity consequences. Diagnostic categories are not simply placed on passive people. They interact with self-understanding, family decisions, advocacy, media representation, and changing scientific ideas. Autistic self-advocates have used neurological language to challenge shame, claim rights, build community, and question who gets defined as normal. At the same time, Ortega warns that even an affirming brain-based identity can become reductive if it suggests that a person is only a brain or that all autistic people share one political or personal experience. This tension matters to me: rejecting deficit-only thinking should not lead to a new single story.

The media source *Autism: Insight from Inside* (Infobase, 2016) made that tension concrete. Participants described how a diagnosis can unlock funding, services, language, and community while also bringing stigma and lowered expectations. They emphasized that people with the same diagnosis may have very different sensory experiences, communication profiles, interests, and support needs. Temple Grandin's emphasis on developing areas of strength was useful because it shifted attention from fixing a child to discovering possibilities. Still, one successful autistic person's story cannot stand in for an entire spectrum. The value of the film was the collection of different perspectives and its insistence that support begins by trying to understand how the world is experienced from inside.

The NEA guide moves this question from description to classroom design. It argues that diagnostic descriptions do not tell the whole story and defines inclusion as more than physical placement: students should be valued members of the classroom, participate in instruction and reciprocal relationships, and receive the supports they need (National Education Association [NEA], 2014/2020). The guide's discussion of behavior is especially important. Movement, repetition, avoidance, refusal, or leaving an activity may be treated as meaningless or defiant, but these actions can communicate sensory overload, anxiety, pain, frustration, a need for predictability, or a lack of control. When a teacher changes the question from "How do I stop this behavior?" to "What might this student be communicating, and what barrier is present?" the student's difference is understood differently.

My elementary technology teaching placement strengthened this lesson. Across K–5 technology, coding, and robotics activities, I repeatedly saw how competence can be hidden by the design surrounding the academic task. A learner may understand the underlying idea but struggle with a long verbal direction, an unfamiliar login path, reading load, device navigation, noise, waiting, peer control of the device, or uncertainty about what finished work should look like. When I used visible first/next/last sequences, modeled the exact tool path, broke tasks into smaller steps, provided examples, offered choices, and allowed additional processing time, participation changed. I did not need to know or assume a diagnosis to recognize that the original environment was producing incomplete evidence about what the learner could do.

That placement experience also changed how I interpret independence. Support should not mean doing the task for a student or making every decision on the student's behalf. The strongest scaffolds make the goal visible, provide an accessible entry point, and then preserve opportunities for choice, productive struggle, communication, and repair. A visual sequence, partner role, sensory option, communication board, model, or quiet reset should help reveal competence rather than replace it. This is the connection I now see between communication and positive behavioral support: both are systems for increasing access and agency.

As a neurodivergent educator, this question is not abstract for me. I know that a person can be described very differently depending on whether an institution notices effort, context, and strengths or only records missed deadlines, uneven performance, communication differences, or difficulty navigating an inaccessible process. Lived experience does not replace professional knowledge, but professional knowledge is incomplete without lived experience. This makes humility part of my teaching practice. I should not assume that outward behavior gives me direct access to another person's thinking.

Developmental differences must also be understood alongside multiple identities. An autistic learner may also be multilingual, racialized, gender-diverse, economically marginalized, physically disabled, or part of a family and culture with its own language for disability and support. Those identities affect who is noticed, who is believed, who receives an evaluation, which behavior is punished, and what services are available. Treating autism as an isolated variable can therefore reproduce inequity. A culturally responsive understanding requires student voice, family knowledge, attention to language and community, and awareness that the same behavior may be interpreted differently depending on who displays it.

This connects directly to my Puzzle Plan because diagnostic systems, school systems, and technology systems all create evidence about people. A shallow system records only the label, error, delay, referral, or behavior. A richer system also records the task demands, sensory environment, communication options, language, tool access, peer dynamics, prior knowledge, student preference, and what changed when support was offered. The quality of the evidence shapes the quality of the response. If the evidence is deficit-only, the intervention will likely ask the student to become easier for the system. If the evidence includes context and agency, educators can redesign the system to become more responsive to the student.

My answer to the essential question is that the world understands developmental differences through labels, stories, relationships, institutions, environments, and the voices it chooses to treat as credible. A label can open access, but it becomes harmful when adults let it replace curiosity. In my future classroom, I want to combine diagnostic information with direct observation, student communication, family knowledge, sensory and environmental information, peer dynamics, and collaboration with specialists. The goal is not to make every learner communicate, move, or participate in the same way. The goal is to build a classroom where students with autism and other complex support needs can communicate, make choices, demonstrate competence, belong, and be believed.
