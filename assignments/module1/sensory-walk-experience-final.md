# Sensory Walk Experience — Final Submission

**Course:** EDE 448 — Communication and Positive Behavioral Supports for Students with Autism and Other Complex Needs  
**Public space examined:** Pine Brook Elementary School IGNITE classroom  
**Observation context:** Spring 2026 student-teaching placement  

## Summary of the Experience

For this sensory walk, I examined the IGNITE classroom at Pine Brook Elementary School, where I completed my student-teaching placement in computer science and inclusion. I approached the classroom as a sensory detective by retracing a typical student experience from entering the room, receiving directions, moving into a hands-on activity, using technology and materials, and transitioning to cleanup or the next class.

The Guggenheim sensory-detective resource explains that sensory-friendly environments should be designed so that neurodivergent visitors can participate fully rather than simply endure the space. It also encourages observers to examine the entire experience from beginning to end. Michigan’s Developmental Disabilities Institute checklist adds practical questions about noise, bright lights, temperature, crowds, alternative routes, sensory maps, and quiet spaces. I used those questions to consider not only what was present in the classroom, but also what students were required to filter, tolerate, or communicate in order to participate.

Overall, I found the IGNITE classroom to be **partially sensory-friendly**. It included several strong instructional and access supports, especially visual directions, flexible participation, movement opportunities, and hands-on choices. At the same time, the room could become difficult for a child who is sensitive to overlapping sound, visual activity, rapid transitions, or crowded material use. The most important finding was that sensory-friendliness changed depending on how the space was being used. During calm, structured instruction, the room offered strong access. During active building, robotics, Chromebook use, and cleanup, the sensory load increased quickly.

## Evidence of Sensory-Friendly Features

### Visual structure and predictability

One of the strongest features was the use of visual and step-by-step instructional supports. The IGNITE curriculum includes picture-based choice boards, visual representations of classroom expectations, and step cards that break activities into smaller parts. These supports make the sequence of an activity more predictable and reduce the amount of spoken language a student must hold in working memory.

This aligns with the sensory-detective idea that an environment should help visitors anticipate what will happen. It also supports students who process language more slowly, use AAC, need repeated directions, or become anxious when expectations are unclear. In this setting, visual support was not an extra accommodation added after difficulty occurred; it was part of the instructional design.

### Flexible participation and regulation

The classroom also supported different ways of participating. Movement and sensory breaks were available, students could use different materials, and instruction could be adjusted through modeling, simplified steps, or alternative response methods. One example from my placement involved a student who was more comfortable keeping a laptop nearby while also participating in a building activity. Rather than treating the laptop as refusal or distraction, we allowed it to remain part of the student’s regulation and participation. The student was then able to engage with the blocks in a productive way.

That example reflects our course theme that behavior is communication. The better question was not, “How do we make the student participate in the standard way?” It was, “What is this student communicating, and what access condition will support meaningful participation?”

### Tactile choice and hands-on learning

The room included many forms of hands-on learning, such as blocks, LEGO materials, robotics tools, craft supplies, and other design materials. This could be supportive for children who learn through movement, touch, building, and experimentation. Students were not limited to listening or completing print-based work. They could test ideas physically, manipulate objects, and use technology as another form of expression.

This was sensory-friendly when students had choices and materials were organized clearly. It allowed children to use their strengths and interests rather than being judged only through verbal or written performance.

## Evidence of Sensory Barriers

### Overlapping sound

The Michigan checklist asks whether a setting has identified sources of sensory difficulty such as noise and crowds. During active IGNITE lessons, several sounds could occur at once: student conversations, Chromebook audio, robot movement, blocks or building materials touching tables, teacher directions, and cleanup activity. Each sound by itself might be manageable, but the combined effect could make it difficult for a child to locate the teacher’s voice or remain regulated.

A child who covered their ears, stopped working, repeated a question, moved away from the group, or refused to transition could easily be misread as inattentive or noncompliant. A sensory-detective interpretation would consider whether the sound level had exceeded the child’s ability to process information.

### Visual and organizational load

The classroom’s technology, displays, bins, construction materials, student projects, and activity stations supported rich learning, but they also created many possible points of attention. During hands-on lessons, multiple materials could be visible at the same time. For some students, this supported curiosity. For others, it could make it harder to identify the first step, locate the correct materials, or ignore objects unrelated to the immediate task.

The room became more sensory-friendly when materials were pre-sorted, only necessary tools were placed on the table, and the next step was shown visually. This demonstrated that the same environment could either support or overload a child depending on how the activity was prepared.

### Transitions and access to a reset space

Transitions were another possible barrier. Different grade levels rotated through the IGNITE room, and students had to enter, orient themselves, begin a task, stop, clean up, and leave within a limited instructional block. The checklist asks whether a setting offers an alternative route, sensory map, or quiet area. The classroom had flexibility and movement breaks, but I did not observe a clearly labeled sensory map or a consistently designated low-sensory reset area within the immediate instructional space.

Without a visible reset option, a child may need to leave the activity completely or depend on an adult to recognize overload. That makes access reactive rather than proactive.

## Overall Assessment

I would rate the IGNITE classroom as **sensory-supportive in its teaching practices but not yet fully sensory-designed as a physical environment**. Its strongest features were the adults’ willingness to interpret behavior as communication, the use of visual supports, flexible participation, and multiple ways to engage with learning. These practices reduced barriers and helped students participate without having to perform competence in one narrow way.

The main limitation was that access depended heavily on adult responsiveness and lesson preparation. When materials were organized, directions were visual, and choices were available, the classroom worked well. When noise, movement, technology, materials, and transitions accumulated, the room placed more demands on students’ sensory regulation. A fully sensory-friendly environment should not require a child to reach visible distress before support becomes available.

This connects to the course’s larger question about how the world comes to understand developmental differences. If educators observe only the visible behavior, a sensory response may be labeled as defiance, distraction, or inability. If educators collect richer evidence about noise, visual load, transition demands, communication, and regulation, they are more likely to identify the actual access barrier. Through the Puzzle Plan and EQUITAS lens, the goal is to separate an environmental barrier from a thinking barrier and to redesign the conditions before judging the child.

## Suggestions for Improvement

### 1. Create a visual sensory preview for the classroom

The classroom could provide a one-page sensory story or map showing what students may see, hear, touch, and do during an IGNITE lesson. It could identify the entrance, work areas, technology stations, material bins, cleanup routine, and available break location. Simple icons could warn students when robots, videos, group discussion, or noisy building materials may be used. This would increase predictability before students enter the room.

### 2. Establish a clearly identified low-sensory reset area

A small area of the classroom could be consistently identified as a regulation space with reduced visual clutter, comfortable seating, and access to tools such as noise-reducing headphones, visual timers, or simple sensory objects. Students should be able to use it without the space being treated as punishment or exclusion. The goal would be to support a brief reset followed by re-entry into the activity when the student is ready.

### 3. Reduce sensory load during transitions and hands-on work

Materials could be introduced in stages rather than placed out all at once. Teachers could use a visual countdown before cleanup, lower unnecessary Chromebook volume, provide headphones when audio is needed, and offer a quieter starting position for students who need it. Clear start-and-stop signals should be paired with visual directions so that children do not have to rely only on spoken instructions during noisy moments.

## Reflection

This sensory walk reinforced that a classroom can be inclusive in its values while still containing sensory barriers. The IGNITE classroom already showed important strengths because students were given visual structure, choice, hands-on entry points, and flexible ways to participate. The next step is to make sensory access even more visible, predictable, and built into the physical environment.

The central lesson for me is that the environment is part of every behavior we observe. When a child struggles, educators should not begin by asking how to make the child tolerate more. We should first ask what the child may be hearing, seeing, feeling, or trying to communicate, and then redesign the environment so that participation is possible with dignity, agency, and genuine belonging.
