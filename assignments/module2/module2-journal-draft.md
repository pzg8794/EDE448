# Module 2 Journal Draft

## Prompt

How can support strategies respect dignity, consent, and neurodiversity?

## Submission Draft

Support strategies respect dignity, consent, and neurodiversity when they begin from the assumption that students are communicating something meaningful. In school settings, behavior is often treated as a problem to stop. This module pushes me to ask a different question: what is the student trying to access, avoid, express, protect, or regulate, and how can the environment change so the student has a more dignified way to communicate that need?

The Prevent-Teach-Reinforce model is helpful because it organizes support around prevention, instruction, and reinforcement. I understand prevention as more than stopping a behavior before it happens. Prevention means designing the classroom so students can understand expectations, access materials, predict transitions, and communicate before they are overwhelmed. Teaching means explicitly supporting the skill or communication pathway the student needs, not assuming the student already knows how to ask for help, request a break, join a group, repair conflict, or navigate a confusing task. Reinforcement should not be a bribe for compliance. It should strengthen meaningful participation, self-advocacy, and replacement skills that actually work for the student.

Dignity matters because some behavior plans make the adult comfortable while making the student smaller. A plan can look "successful" if the student becomes quieter, but that does not necessarily mean the student is supported. A dignity-centered plan asks whether the student has more agency, more communication, more belonging, and more access to learning. This is especially important for autistic and neurodivergent students because behaviors such as movement, silence, avoidance, scripting, sensory seeking, or intense focus may be part of regulation or communication rather than defiance.

Consent matters because students should not be forced into support systems that ignore their bodymind needs. In a classroom, consent can look like offering choices, explaining why a support is being used, asking what helps, respecting communication preferences, and avoiding public shaming. It can also mean giving students ways to opt into help: visual break cards, private check-ins, calm corners, choice boards, partner options, or a signal that they need clarification.

Neurodiversity matters because support should not be designed to make every student appear neurotypical. Instead, support should reduce barriers and expand participation. In my technology-rich teaching context, I have seen how students' competence can be hidden by login friction, reading load, unfamiliar interfaces, language demands, or executive-function demands. A student may look off task when the real issue is that the task sequence is not visible, the tool is confusing, or the student needs a peer role that fits their strengths. This connects to the Puzzle Plan because systems often misread people when they collect shallow evidence. In the classroom, shallow evidence might be "student refused." Better evidence asks: what happened before, what support was available, what communication options existed, what sensory or social load was present, and what would make the next attempt more accessible?

For my future classroom, I want support strategies to be proactive, collaborative, and transparent. I would rather build routines, visual supports, sensory options, and relationship-based repair practices before students are in crisis. When individualized support is needed, I want to include the student, family, and support team as much as possible. The goal is not perfect behavior. The goal is a classroom where students can communicate needs, make choices, repair harm, take academic risks, and be understood without losing dignity.
