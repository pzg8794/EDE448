# Positive Classroom Support Plan Draft

- Describe a positive classroom support plan for a future classroom.

## Submission Draft

My positive classroom support plan is designed for an inclusive, technology-rich upper-elementary or middle school classroom where students may vary in communication, sensory needs, executive functioning, language, prior technology access, and comfort with group work. The goal is not to make every student behave the same way. The goal is to build a classroom where expectations are clear, supports are visible, students have meaningful choices, and behavior is treated as communication.

## 1. Classroom Values And Expectations

I would begin with a short set of collaboratively built classroom values:

- We communicate needs respectfully.
- We make space for different bodies, brains, languages, and communication styles.
- We repair harm instead of hiding it.
- We ask for help early.
- We protect everyone's right to learn.

Instead of posting rules as only adult commands, I would co-create examples with students. For each value, we would name what it looks like, sounds like, and feels like during whole-group lessons, partner work, technology tasks, transitions, and independent work. This connects to positive behavior support because students need explicit expectations, but it also protects dignity because the expectations are framed around community access rather than control.

## 2. Predictable Routines And Visual Supports

Students would have access to:

- a visual agenda
- step-by-step task cards
- "what to do when stuck" prompts
- transition warnings
- role cards for group work
- a private help signal
- a calm/break option

These routines support students before difficulty escalates. In a PTR frame, they are prevention strategies: they reduce uncertainty, language load, and executive-function demand. They also help adults distinguish between refusal and confusion.

## 3. Communication And Choice

A positive support plan should give students multiple ways to communicate. I would use verbal options, written options, visuals, gestures, AAC if used by the student, and low-stigma signals such as a help card or break card. Students should be able to communicate:

- I need help.
- I need more time.
- I need a break.
- I do not understand the step.
- I want to work alone / with a partner.
- I am ready to return.

Choice would be built into assignments when possible: choice of role, tool, response mode, order of tasks, seating, or partner structure. Choice-making supports self-advocacy and aligns with the course outcome of promoting independence.

## 4. Sensory And Regulation Supports

The classroom would include sensory-aware options such as flexible seating, movement breaks, predictable noise expectations, visual clutter checks, and access to a low-sensory reset space. I would not treat every movement, silence, or break request as avoidance. Instead, I would ask whether the student is regulating, processing, or communicating a need.

## 5. Reinforcement And Feedback

I would use reinforcement to strengthen meaningful participation rather than compliance alone. I would notice and name:

- using a communication support
- asking for help respectfully
- trying a strategy before escalating
- returning after a break
- including a peer
- explaining thinking
- repairing conflict

Feedback would be specific and private when correction is needed. Public behavior charts or public shaming would not fit this plan because they can damage dignity and belonging.

## 6. Restorative Repair

When harm or disruption happens, the response would be calm, brief, and repair-focused:

1. What happened?
2. What was the need or barrier?
3. Who was affected?
4. What support or repair is needed now?
5. What should we adjust before next time?

This keeps accountability while still treating behavior as information.

## 7. Family And Team Collaboration

Families, students, paraprofessionals, therapists, and other team members should be part of support planning. Families often know sensory triggers, communication preferences, recovery strategies, and strengths that may not be visible at school. Collaboration also reduces the risk of creating a plan that works only for adults in one setting.

## Reading Connections

Dunlap et al.'s Prevent-Teach-Reinforce model supports the prevention/teaching/reinforcement structure of this plan. Jorgensen's work on communicative competence and authentic inclusion supports the idea that students with complex support needs need real participation, not just physical placement. Denning and Moody's behavioral intervention frame helps connect proactive supports to autism and inclusion. Zacarian and Silverstone's family/community partnership emphasis supports the collaboration part of the plan. Together, these readings push me toward support that is proactive, respectful, and relationship-centered.

## Conclusion

This positive classroom support plan reflects my developing teaching identity. I want my classroom to be structured enough that students feel safe and predictable, but flexible enough that students do not have to erase their differences to belong. A strong support plan should help students communicate, self-advocate, participate, and repair. It should also help me read behavior more carefully as evidence of access, context, and unmet needs.
