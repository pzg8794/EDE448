## Draft Response

My main takeaway from Module 2 is that positive support has to be judged by what it gives students, not only by what it stops. A support plan might reduce a visible behavior, but if it also reduces student voice, choice, or belonging, then it is not fully positive. A dignity-centered plan should help students communicate needs, participate more successfully, repair conflict, and access learning.

The Prevent-Teach-Reinforce structure helps me think about this more clearly. Prevention asks what can be changed before a pattern escalates: routines, visuals, transitions, sensory load, task clarity, peer roles, or adult prompts. Teaching asks what skill or communication pathway the student needs: asking for help, requesting a break, naming confusion, joining a group, or returning after frustration. Reinforcement should strengthen meaningful skills and self-advocacy, not just quiet compliance.

This module also helped me connect neurodiversity to classroom systems. Respecting neurodiversity does not mean removing all expectations. It means designing expectations and supports in ways that do not assume there is only one acceptable way to communicate, regulate, attend, or participate. A student may need movement, AAC, a visual sequence, a quiet cue, a partner role, or additional processing time. Those supports are not shortcuts; they are access.

In my future classroom, I want to use behavior data carefully. I do not want to record only what went "wrong." I want to notice what happened before, what support was available, what the student communicated, what changed afterward, and what the environment might need next time. That connects to the larger theme of this course: behavior is communication, and support should make communication easier to understand.
