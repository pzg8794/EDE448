## Support Plan

### 1. Presume Competence And Build Access

The plan begins with the assumption that Student A has something meaningful to communicate across the day. The team will not wait for perfect device use before offering robust communication. Student A's communication system will be available during academic, social, sensory, and transition routines.

### 2. Communication Modes

Student A will have access to multiple communication modes:

- primary AAC system: **[insert]**
- backup low-tech board or printed core vocabulary
- break/help cards
- yes/no or choice response options
- gesture/body communication notes shared with staff
- daily home-school communication log

The backup system matters because communication access should not disappear if a device battery dies, the internet fails, or the student is too dysregulated to navigate the full system.

### 3. Core Vocabulary And Useful Phrases

The team will prioritize words and phrases Student A can use across many routines:

- help
- stop
- more
- finished
- wait
- break
- I need
- I do not understand
- my turn
- different
- yes/no
- I like / I do not like
- I feel
- I want to tell home
- I meant something else

Adults will also add activity-specific vocabulary for current units, peers, locations, and classroom routines.

### 4. Classroom Routine Implementation

| Routine | Communication Opportunity | Adult Support |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Arrival | greet, choose first task, share home note | Model greeting/choice; check home-school log |
| Whole-group lesson | comment, answer, ask for repetition | Provide wait time; offer AAC-compatible response options |
| Group work | choose role, ask for help, reject/accept materials | Teach peers to wait, read AAC output, and confirm meaning |
| Transitions | request break, ask where to go, signal not ready | Use visual schedule and transition countdown |
| Lunch/recess | choose peer/activity, request space, comment socially | Make AAC available outside classroom, not only during academics |
| Dismissal | share one thing from the day, ask home question | Complete student-centered daily communication log |

### 5. Home-School Communication Log

The daily log will include:

- one thing Student A communicated
- one successful support
- one barrier or pattern noticed
- one preference/choice from the day
- one question or message for home
- family response section

This should not become a behavior report that only documents problems. It should preserve communication, preferences, and successes.

### 6. Communication Partner Expectations

Adults and peers will:

- keep AAC available and within reach
- model without demanding immediate imitation
- wait long enough for processing
- respond to all communication attempts
- confirm meaning respectfully
- avoid speaking over the student
- avoid removing AAC as a consequence
- treat refusal/protest as valid communication

### 7. Data And Reflection

The team will collect simple data:

- number of routines where AAC was available
- types of messages communicated
- prompts needed
- communication breakdowns
- successful repairs
- family notes

The purpose of data is to improve access, not to prove whether the student deserves communication.

## Submission Draft

My communication support plan focuses on a non-speaking or minimally speaking student in an inclusive upper-elementary classroom. I designed the plan around the idea that meaningful communication should be available across the day, not only during speech sessions or isolated academic tasks.

The first part of the plan is access. Student A should have a primary AAC system, a low-tech backup, visual supports, break/help cards, and a daily home-school communication log. This matters because students with complex communication needs should not lose communication access because a device is unavailable, an adult is absent, or a routine changes.

The second part is classroom implementation. I would embed communication opportunities into arrival, whole-group lessons, group work, transitions, lunch/recess, and dismissal. For example, during group work, Student A should be able to choose a role, ask for help, comment on the task, reject a material, or request a break. During dismissal, Student A should have a way to share something from the day with home. Communication should include more than answering adult questions.

The third part is communication partner training. Adults and peers need to know how to wait, model, respond, confirm meaning, and avoid speaking over the student. AAC should never be removed as a consequence. Refusal and protest should be treated as valid communication because dignity includes the right to say no or ask for something different.

The home-school log is an important part of the plan because families often know communication patterns that school may miss. The log should include successful communication, preferences, supports that worked, barriers, and messages between home and school. I would avoid making it only a behavior log because that would narrow the student's identity to problems.

This plan connects to the Module 3 readings because AAC and communication support require more than a tool. Students need access, modeling, vocabulary, communication partners, and real opportunities to use language. My future classroom should be a communication-rich environment where students with complex needs can influence routines, relationships, academics, and support decisions.
