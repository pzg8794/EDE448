## Discussion Post Draft

For my AAC review, I looked at **TD Snap** by Tobii Dynavox. TD Snap is a communication software platform designed for people with speech and language disabilities, including people with autism, cerebral palsy, aphasia, and other complex communication needs. What stood out to me is that it is not only one fixed vocabulary layout. It includes different page sets, such as Core First, Motor Plan, Express, Text, Scanning, and Aphasia, so a team can choose a setup that better fits the communicator's needs and access method.

One strength of TD Snap is flexibility. It can support touch, switch, and eye-gaze access, which matters because students with complex needs may have very different motor, sensory, and visual access profiles. I also appreciate that the tool includes both symbol-supported and text-based options. That makes it easier to think about AAC as something that can grow with the student rather than as a single static board.

Another strength is that TD Snap can be connected to classroom participation. A student could use it to request a break, answer academic questions, comment during a read-aloud, participate in morning meeting, choose a peer role, ask for help, or share information between home and school. For me, the most important question is not "Can the app speak?" but "Does the communication system help the student influence what happens in the classroom?"

There are also challenges. A tool like TD Snap requires training, time, modeling, and consistency across adults. If only one specialist knows how to use it, the student may not have real communication access all day. Another challenge is vocabulary planning. Adults may be tempted to include only behavior-management phrases or basic requests, but students need access to robust language: feelings, questions, refusals, comments, jokes, academic words, people, places, and repair phrases like "I meant something else." Cost, device access, family training, and school-team coordination can also become barriers.

I would use TD Snap in a classroom as part of a full communication plan, not as a device handed to a student in isolation. I would want the team to model use without pressure, keep the device available across routines, teach peers how to wait and respond respectfully, and make sure the student can use it for more than compliance. For example, during a group STEM task, the student should be able to choose a role, ask for help, reject a material, make a prediction, comment on what happened, or request a sensory break.

My overall review is that TD Snap has strong potential when it is implemented as a meaningful communication system. Its flexibility and access-method options are major benefits. The biggest risk is not the tool itself, but shallow implementation. If adults do not model, listen, customize, and honor the student's communication, even a strong AAC tool can become another symbol of adult control. If implemented well, TD Snap can support agency, social connection, academic participation, and dignity.
