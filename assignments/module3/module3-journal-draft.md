# Module 3 Journal Draft

## Prompt

What does meaningful communication look like for students with complex needs?

## Submission Draft

Meaningful communication for students with complex needs is communication that is understood, respected, and acted on. It is not limited to spoken words. It can include AAC, gestures, facial expression, eye gaze, body movement, behavior, objects, pictures, typing, signs, partner-assisted scanning, vocalizations, and silence. The key question is whether the student's communication changes what happens next in a way that gives the student more agency.

This module is helping me separate communication from compliance. A student may be communicating even when the message is not convenient for adults. Refusing, leaving, repeating, reaching, pushing away, shutting down, or becoming upset can all communicate something about access, sensory load, comprehension, fatigue, pain, anxiety, preference, or unmet support needs. The educator's responsibility is not only to respond to the surface behavior, but to build a more reliable communication pathway.

AAC is important because it can make language more available, but AAC should not be treated as a device-only solution. A student needs a communication system, not just a tool. That system includes modeling, vocabulary, wait time, communication partners, access method, classroom routines, family collaboration, and opportunities to use communication for real purposes. If AAC is only used for requesting snacks or answering adult questions, the student is still being limited. Meaningful communication should include protesting, commenting, asking, joking, choosing, refusing, sharing information, asking for help, participating in academics, and building relationships.

The readings on AAC and communication supports connect directly to dignity. Students with complex needs should not have to prove readiness for communication. They should have access to robust communication from the beginning. In a general education classroom, that means communication supports need to be embedded into real routines: morning arrival, transitions, group work, lunch, specials, technology tasks, home-school communication, and academic discussions.

My Puzzle Plan connection is that communication support is a form of evidence justice. If a student does not have an accessible way to communicate, the school may collect inaccurate evidence about the student's understanding, behavior, and needs. A student may be labeled as disengaged when the real issue is that the communication system is too narrow. A more just system would ask: what modes of communication are available, who is listening, what counts as a response, and how do we know the student had a genuine opportunity to be understood?

In my future classroom, meaningful communication will require three commitments. First, I need to presume competence while still providing explicit support. Second, I need to teach peers and adults to be communication partners, not just observers. Third, I need to design routines where communication has real consequences: students can make choices, influence activities, ask questions, reject options, request changes, and share ideas.

Meaningful communication looks like a student being able to affect their world. It looks like adults slowing down enough to notice. It looks like AAC being modeled without pressure. It looks like families and school teams sharing what works. It looks like behavior being interpreted with curiosity. Most of all, it looks like students with complex needs being treated as people with something important to say.
