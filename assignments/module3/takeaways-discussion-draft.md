# Module 3 Discussion: Takeaways

## Prompt Focus

1. What are some takeaways from this module for you?
2. What do you plan to incorporate or explore further after this course?

## Ready-To-Submit Response

My biggest takeaway from this module is that meaningful communication has to be measured by agency, not by whether a student technically has a tool available. A student may have an AAC device, picture system, communication board, or assistive technology, but the deeper question is whether the student can use it to influence their day, relationships, learning, and support. Communication should include choosing, refusing, commenting, asking questions, repairing misunderstandings, sharing feelings, participating academically, and building social connection.

Another takeaway is that AAC success depends on communication partners and environments. AAC does not work only because a student has access to a device or board. It works when adults and peers model language, wait, respond, confirm meaning respectfully, and keep the system available across routines. If a student's communication system is removed, limited to basic requests, used only during speech sessions, or treated as inconvenient during busy classroom moments, then the system can accidentally silence the student.

The module also helped me connect AAC to behavior support. When communication access is blocked, behavior may become the message. That does not mean every behavior has a simple interpretation, but it does mean educators should respond with curiosity before blame. I want to keep asking what the student may be communicating, what access barrier is present, and what support would give the student a safer or clearer way to communicate next time.

The Communication Support Plan pushed me to think about home-school communication as more than a behavior report. A daily log should document what the student communicated, what supports worked, what barriers appeared, what the family wants school to know, and what the student might want carried across settings. That kind of log can become a bridge rather than a list of problems.

After this course, I plan to incorporate communication support as part of Tier 1 inclusive design. I want classrooms where students have multiple ways to understand, respond, ask for help, participate, take breaks, refuse, and repair misunderstandings. I also want to keep exploring AAC tool selection, low-tech backup systems, peer communication partner routines, family-centered communication logs, and assistive technology that does not make students feel conspicuous or burdensome. In my larger Puzzle Plan work, this module gives me language for one of my central commitments: schools should collect better evidence about student thinking and communication instead of mistaking speech, speed, or compliance for competence.
