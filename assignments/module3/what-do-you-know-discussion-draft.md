# Module 3 Discussion: What Do You Know?

## Prompt Focus

1. What do you already know about assistive technology and AAC for students with disabilities?
2. What are you hoping to explore and learn about in this module?

## Ready-To-Submit Response

Before this module, I understand assistive technology and AAC as access systems, not as extras, rewards, or last-resort interventions. AAC can include high-tech tools, low-tech boards, picture symbols, core vocabulary, gestures, eye gaze, writing, objects, switches, partner-assisted scanning, speech-generating devices, and other ways students communicate across the day. The most important starting point for me is that a student does not stop communicating because adults do not yet understand the message. If the support system is too narrow, the barrier is in the environment, not in the student's worth or intelligence.

I also know that AAC is not only a device. A device can be powerful, but meaningful communication depends on the whole context around the student: modeling, wait time, vocabulary access, family knowledge, peer response, sensory load, staff consistency, backup systems, and whether adults actually honor what the student communicates. If AAC is only used for requesting snacks, answering adult questions, or proving compliance, then the student still does not have full communication access. Communication should include choosing, refusing, commenting, joking, asking questions, repairing misunderstandings, sharing feelings, participating academically, and building relationships.

I am coming into this module wanting to learn how to make AAC and assistive technology usable in real classroom routines, not just listed on a plan. I want to explore how teachers choose tools, model language without taking over, create low-tech backup options, teach peers to be respectful communication partners, and build routines where students can use AAC during arrival, transitions, group work, lunch, recess, academic discussion, and dismissal. I also want to understand how daily communication logs and team planning can preserve student voice instead of turning communication into a behavior report.

This connects directly to my course theme of communication as access-to-agency. For me, the key question is not "Does the student have a tool?" but "Can the student use support to affect what happens next?" If AAC and assistive technology help adults listen more carefully, reveal competence, and protect dignity, then they become part of equity work and not just special education equipment.
